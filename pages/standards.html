Acceptance guidelines and handling process

These standards are what make the concept feel like a bureau and not a public revenge wall.

Acceptance guidelines Publication standards

1. What CRB will handle

CRB is intended for marketplace matters tied to a product, service, appointment, rental, delivery, pickup, payment dispute, chargeback, nonpayment, damage claim, or similar business-to-customer issue.

2. What CRB will not handle

  • Employment or workplace disputes
  • Medical, family, or domestic matters
  • Unsupported criminal accusations
  • Threats, retaliation, or abusive language
  • Complaints that do not involve a marketplace relationship

3. Filing standards

A filing must come from a verified business, identify the reported party with enough detail for bureau review, and include a date, reference number, and concise summary of what occurred. CRB may request supporting records before a file moves into notice or publication review.

4. Notice and response process

After a filing is accepted, the reported party can be notified and given a response window. This demo uses a bureau-style cadence: accepted matters are typically processed within two business days, a response is generally requested within 14 days, and many matters are updated or closed within about 30 days.

5. Publication levels and statuses

Some files are never published. Others are published only in summary form. A file can show as pending review, answered, unresolved, unanswered, dispute open, limited summary, or closed depending on the record history and supporting information on file.

6. Business listing and membership review

Businesses can request a public listing and separately apply for bureau membership. Listed businesses can maintain public contact information and respond to published matters. Member businesses may also submit new reviews and use filing tools.

7. Corrections and disputes

Correction requests, response statements, and disputes stay attached to the same record. CRB can reduce public detail, update status, or close a matter based on the information received during review.

Typical handling timeline

Working model
  • Within 2 business days Accepted filings move into review, notice, or document screening.
  • Within 14 days The reported party is typically asked to respond.
  • Within 30 days Many matters are updated, published, reduced, or closed.

Publication caution

Important

Public file text should stay factual, concise, and tied to the marketplace issue. CRB does not present files as court findings, government actions, or credit reports.
